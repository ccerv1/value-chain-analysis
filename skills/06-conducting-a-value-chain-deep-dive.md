# Skill 6: Conducting a Value Chain Deep Dive

## What It Is
The complete methodology for conducting a value chain analysis from scratch — from scoping the engagement through fieldwork, analysis, and final recommendations. Skills 1-5 taught the individual analytical techniques. This skill puts them together into a coherent process. The methodology below applies to any agricultural commodity or sector. The Coffee Appendix at the end provides sector-specific data sources, conversion factors, and reference points.

## The Process

### 1. Scoping
Before you start analyzing, define what you are analyzing and why.

- **Product**: What commodity or product? Be specific. "Coffee" is too broad — washed Arabica in Rwanda is a different analysis than Robusta in Vietnam.
- **Geographic boundaries**: National? Regional? A specific supply corridor?
- **Time period**: Current snapshot? Trend analysis over 5 years? Pre- and post-policy change?
- **Stages**: The full chain (farm to cup), or a subset (farm to export)? Most development-focused VCAs focus on farm to export, where data challenges are greatest and intervention leverage is highest.
- **Commissioner and purpose**: Who wants this analysis and why? A donor designing a program needs different outputs than a government writing a national coffee strategy or a private company evaluating a sourcing decision. The purpose shapes the analysis.

### 2. Desk Research
Before you go to the field, know what is already known.

- **Trade statistics**: COMTRADE for export volumes and values by country, national statistical agencies for production data
- **Academic literature**: Published VCAs in the same sector or country, agricultural economics journals, working papers from research institutions (IFPRI, CIRAD, World Bank)
- **NGO and donor reports**: Program evaluations, baseline studies, market assessments. Organizations like TechnoServe, Oxfam, USAID, and GIZ have published extensive coffee VCA work.
- **Government data**: National coffee board statistics, agricultural census data, policy documents
- **Industry sources**: ICO (International Coffee Organization), trade associations, specialty coffee organizations (SCA)
- **Price history**: Futures market data (ICE), physical price data from exporters or traders

Desk research gives you a hypothesis — a preliminary map, some benchmark numbers, and a set of questions to investigate in the field. It is not a substitute for fieldwork.

### 3. Fieldwork
This is where the analysis gets real.

**Stakeholder identification.** Cast a wide net. Your interview list should include:
- Farmers (different sizes, different regions, different quality levels)
- Processors (wet mill operators, dry mill operators)
- Traders and collectors (formal and informal)
- Exporters
- Government officials (agricultural ministry, export authority, tax authority)
- Input suppliers (fertilizer, seedlings, tools)
- Transport companies
- NGOs and donor-funded programs operating in the sector
- Industry associations
- Extension agents

**Interview design.** Keep interviews semi-structured. Have a topic guide, not a rigid questionnaire. Key questions for each actor:
- What do you do? (Role in the chain)
- Who do you buy from and sell to? (Relationships)
- What do you pay and what do you charge? (Prices and margins)
- What are your costs? (Cost structure)
- What has changed in the last 3-5 years? (Trends)
- What are the biggest problems you face? (Constraints)

**Data collection.** Practical tips from the field:
- Carry a USB drive. Many people have data in Excel or PDF but no easy way to share it digitally.
- Have a simple template ready for price and cost data.
- Offer confidentiality when asking about margins and costs — people are sensitive about this.
- Record quantities in whatever units the interviewee uses, then convert later. Do not try to convert in real time during an interview.
- Take photos of documents, receipts, and price boards when permitted.

**Triangulation.** Never rely on a single source. If a cooperative manager tells you farmers receive 500 RWF/kg cherry, verify with farmers themselves. If an exporter quotes a cost structure, check it against published data. When two independent sources give you the same number, you can trust it. When they diverge significantly, you have found something worth investigating.

### 4. Analysis
Apply Skills 1-5 in sequence:

1. **Map** the value chain (Skill 1): Finalize actor identification, draw the chain, identify parallel channels
2. **Break down** value flows (Skill 2): Build the waterfall from farmer to export, using converted prices (Skill 3)
3. **Benchmark** against peers (Skill 4): Compare yields, prices, farmer share, costs across relevant comparators
4. **Synthesize**: Where are the gaps? Where are the opportunities? What explains the differences you found?
5. **Prioritize recommendations** (Skill 5): Impact-feasibility matrix, segmented by producer type

**Quantitative and qualitative integration.** The numbers (waterfall, benchmarks) tell you what is happening. The interviews tell you why. A VCA that is all numbers lacks explanation. A VCA that is all narratives lacks rigor. The best analyses weave both together: "Farmers capture 54% of the export price (quantitative finding). This is because coffee washing stations charge significant processing fees, which are partly offset by government subsidies (qualitative explanation from interviews)."

### 5. Reporting
Structure your report for the audience:

**For donors and development agencies:**
- Lead with the problem statement and proposed interventions
- Put methodology at the end
- Emphasize impact potential and beneficiary numbers
- Include a clear budget or cost estimate for recommended interventions

**For government officials:**
- Lead with national-level implications (employment, export revenue, rural development)
- Connect recommendations to existing policy frameworks
- Be realistic about institutional capacity

**For private sector:**
- Lead with the business case
- Focus on sourcing risks, quality opportunities, and competitive positioning
- Include market data and trends

**For all audiences:**
- Keep the main report concise (20-30 pages)
- Put detailed data, methodology, and interview notes in appendices
- Use visual summaries: the map, the waterfall, the benchmark chart, the impact-feasibility matrix
- State your assumptions explicitly
- Acknowledge limitations and data gaps

### 6. Common Pitfalls

**Overreliance on official statistics.** Government production figures are often estimates based on outdated census data. Area under cultivation may not reflect reality. Export statistics are more reliable (they are tracked at the port) but still have gaps (informal cross-border trade, smuggling).

**Missing informal channels.** In many countries, a significant share of coffee moves through informal channels that are invisible in official data. Village-level collectors, cross-border trade, and domestic sales through unregistered channels may account for 10-30% of total volume.

**Conversion errors.** The most common technical mistake. Double-check every unit conversion. Have someone else verify your math. A cherry-to-green ratio of 5:1 vs 7:1 changes the farmer share calculation by 30-40%.

**Single-source bias.** A VCA based on interviews with only exporters will tell the exporter's story. A VCA based on interviews with only NGOs will tell the development narrative. Interview actors at every stage and triangulate.

**Confirmation bias.** If you go into the field expecting to find exploitative middlemen, you will find evidence of exploitative middlemen — even if the reality is more nuanced. Be open to surprises. In Vietnam, the middleman economy is highly competitive and delivers 95% of the export price to farmers. That is not the story most people expect.

**Treating the analysis as a one-time exercise.** Value chains change. Prices fluctuate. Policies shift. New actors enter. A VCA is a snapshot, not a permanent truth. Build in a plan for updating the analysis, or at minimum, clearly state the date and conditions under which it was conducted.

## Coffee Appendix

Reference material specific to coffee value chain analysis.

### Data Sources

| Source | What It Provides | URL/Access |
|--------|-----------------|------------|
| ICO (International Coffee Organization) | Production, consumption, prices, trade flows by country | ico.org |
| USDA Foreign Agricultural Service | Production estimates, attaché reports by country, GAIN reports | fas.usda.gov |
| COMTRADE (UN) | Export and import volumes and values, bilateral trade flows | comtrade.un.org |
| National coffee boards | Country-specific production, quality data, registration data | Varies by country (e.g., NAEB in Rwanda, ANACAFE in Guatemala, ECX in Ethiopia) |
| SCA (Specialty Coffee Association) | Quality standards, cupping protocols, market trends | sca.coffee |
| ICE Futures | Arabica "C" contract prices, Robusta London prices | theice.com |
| World Bank | Commodity price data (historical), development indicators | worldbank.org |

### Standard Conversion Factors

| Conversion | Factor | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Cherry to green (Arabica) | ~6:1 to 7:1 | Varies by varietal and agro-climate (including altitude), not processing method. Rwanda ~7:1; most others ~6.25:1 |
| Cherry to green (Robusta) | ~5:1 | Lower ratio, reflecting different bean size and density |
| Cherry to parchment | ~5:1 to 6:1 | Intermediate product (washed process only) |
| Parchment to green | ~1.25:1 | Hulling removes parchment layer |
| Kilograms to pounds | 1 kg = 2.205 lb | |
| Standard bag | 60 kg = 132.28 lb | International jute bag |
| Containers | ~250 bags per 20-ft container | ~15 metric tons |

### Typical Actor Categories

- **Smallholder farmer**: <5 hectares, family labor, diversified crops, sells cherry
- **Estate/plantation**: >50 hectares, hired labor, may process on-site
- **Mobile collector/middleman**: Buys cherry from farmers, aggregates, sells to mill or exporter
- **Coffee washing station (wet mill)**: De-pulps, ferments, washes, dries cherry into parchment
- **Dry mill**: Hulls parchment to green, grades, bags for export
- **Cooperative**: Farmer-owned organization that may operate washing stations, aggregate volume, and export directly
- **Exporter**: Consolidates green coffee, arranges shipping, manages export documentation
- **Trader/importer**: International buyers who purchase FOB and sell to roasters
- **Roaster**: Blends and roasts green coffee for consumer market
- **Retailer**: Sells roasted/ground coffee or brewed coffee to consumers

### Price Benchmarks

- **ICE "C" contract** (New York): Global Arabica benchmark, quoted in US cents per pound. Physical coffee trades at a differential to this price.
- **ICE London**: Global Robusta benchmark, quoted in USD per metric ton.
- **Country differentials**: Colombia typically trades at C + $0.15-0.30 (premium). Ethiopia at C + $0.20-0.50 (premium, varies by grade). Vietnam Robusta trades relative to London. Brazil trades near or slightly below C (large volume, standard quality).

### Quality Grading

- **SCA cupping score**: The standard quality assessment. Scored out of 100. Coffee scoring 80+ is considered "specialty grade."
- **Specialty vs commercial threshold**: 80 points. Below 80 is commercial grade. Above 85 is considered excellent. Above 90 is exceptional and very rare.
- **Quality premiums**: Specialty coffee commands significant premiums over commercial grade — often $0.50-2.00+ per pound above the C price. This premium is the economic engine behind quality-focused interventions.
